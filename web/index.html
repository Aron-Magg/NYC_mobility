NYC Mobility

A guided storytelling page for visitors who want to understand how New York City moves. The story is based on public ridership and safety data, translated into practical guidance for choosing the right mode.

NYC mobility is layered: the subway moves the backbone, buses fill the gaps, bikes thrive on short hops, taxis bridge door-to-door needs, and surface traffic shows the rhythm of the streets. This guide walks you through those layers using the data that is actually available.

The total system peaks in 2019 and dips in 2020, then partially recovers by 2022. In the latest year, subway holds the largest share at roughly one-third, with taxi/FHV and buses close behind (around one quarter each). Citi Bike remains smaller but shows the clearest upward trajectory.

Subway: the backbone

The subway is the fastest cross-city option for visitors. Its ridership is the largest overall, but the data shows a slow decline from early peaks and a sharper dip around 2020. For visitors, the key takeaway is still simple: if you need speed across boroughs, start with the subway.

How to read it

Bars show average annual ridership for the subway. Hover a bar to see the exact value and the year-to-year pattern.

Use this to compare pre-2020 levels with the recent recovery, and to see how the subway still anchors the system overall.

The mini panels compare subway ridership against all other modes in four key years.

In the latest year, the subway still commands the largest share of riders among the four modes, reinforcing its role as the default backbone for longer trips. The bars show that its recovery is slower than the system as a whole, which is a useful cue when planning long cross-city rides during peak periods.

Bus: surface coverage

Bus ridership is comparatively stable across the years, which signals its role as the dependable "connector" mode. It serves short trips, crosstown movement, and neighborhoods with fewer subway stations.

How to read it

The bus series moves within a narrower band than other modes, suggesting a steady baseline of surface travel in the city.

For visitors, this means buses are a consistent option when the subway is inconvenient or when you want a street-level view.

The mini panels show how the bus share compares to the rest of the system in four reference years.

Bus share remains comparable to taxi/FHV, highlighting how surface modes compete for similar trip types. The steady band of ridership makes buses a predictable, city-wide fallback for short trips and neighborhood connections.

Bike & micromobility

Bike ridership grows over time in the dataset, making it the most clearly rising trend among the four modes. For visitors, this reinforces the value of bikes for quick, flexible trips under three kilometers.

Taxi & FHV: door-to-door

Taxi and for-hire vehicles (FHV) grow steadily in the annual averages. They remain close to buses in total volume, suggesting a strong role for direct, on-demand travel.

How to read it

Taxi/FHV ridership climbs from 2013 onward, showing steady demand for direct trips even when other modes fluctuate.

For visitors, this mode is a good choice for luggage, late-night trips, or trips that avoid transfers.

The mini panels compare taxi/FHV ridership against the rest of the system in four reference years.

Taxi/FHV use does not replace the subway at scale, but it competes closely with buses. If speed is not guaranteed and you need convenience, this mode is the most flexible for direct, door-to-door travel.

Walking & safety signals

Walking is the invisible connector between every other mode. The available borough-level dataset focuses on safety outcomes rather than walking volume, so we use collision data as a proxy signal for where walking exposure is highest.

Pedestrian injuries by borough (choropleth)
What this shows

This chart ranks the four boroughs with the highest collision totals and breaks them down by road user type. It does not represent ridership, only safety outcomes recorded in the dataset.

Motorist-related injuries dominate in every borough, while pedestrian and cyclist values remain smaller but still important for visitor safety.

The mini panels summarize the user-type mix for each of the four boroughs.

Color key: green = pedestrians, cyan = cyclists, amber = motorists.

For visitors, the safety data is a reminder to prioritize crossings and to pay extra attention in the boroughs with the highest totals. In the top four boroughs by collision volume (Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx, and Manhattan), the dominant road user type is motorists, with pedestrians and cyclists trailing. This is not a ridership measure, but it is a practical warning signal for walking trips.

Sources

This story uses three primary datasets: annual ridership by mode, collision records by borough, and automated traffic volume counts. The maps use public GeoJSON layers for borough boundaries and service access points.

Core datasets

  • Annual ridership by mode (Subway, Bus, Citi Bike, Taxi/FHV)
  • Collision records by borough with injured/killed by user type
  • Automated traffic volume counts by borough, hour, and corridor
  • Citi Bike tripdata (station flows, timing, and rider mix)
  • GeoJSON layers for boroughs and service points

Traffic dataset source: https://catalog.data.gov/dataset/automated-traffic-volume-counts. Last accessed 2026-01-12 at 14:00.
